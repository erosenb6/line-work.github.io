Dictionary for Building: a Text

in response to Siah Armajani's Dictionary for Building

My father guides me through the city.
Architect cum docent, he shows me the
corners where walls meet and how the sky
touches rooftops.

Corners can conceal many sins; he sees
the mismatched horizontals before I do.

We are pointing to ugly vertices when I
start calculating compositions. Eaves,
dormers, gutters, stucco. A pediment,
balustrade, plaster pilaster.

Each home proclaims its implicit agreement:
each same slatted side, each box and pointed
top. Even as kids we were in agreement: a triangle,
a square, and you have a crayon house.

Homes hold treachery—crevices, an open staircase,
or worse, but hidden. They shelter emptiness,
vacant rooms, unused tupperwares, moments of
solitude. Yet they also breathe and billow, and
they were built with such hope, constructed and
then cultivated, watered and grown like saplings.

Inevitably, house-talk turns historical, and my
father narrates: the imitation-stones convey
rigidity. The triangle tops are overseas echoes
of Parthenons, ancient victory.

Siah sculpts a single chair in miniature,
which stands alone. Alone except the checkered
tile floor—does this make a home?

Siah sees the fragments and their quiet composition.
I want to write books like building, accidentally,
but of course. Ones that sit in foundations who remain
long past their roofs.

My dad has a tendency to pause. I don’t notice
until he’s missing from my side; I look back
there he is: crouched by a building, taking a photo.
I grew up with this alternately egregious and endearing
habit. At 22, I discover fragments of buildings
catch my attention just the same. We end back where
we started, my father and me side-by-side.
